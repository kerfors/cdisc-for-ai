Part 1
What COSMoS leaves to study assembly — the case-completion gap

A clinical "case" of a glucose measurement requires more than the recording template can carry.

The DSS fixes the test-intrinsic dimensions (specimen, method, scale, unit, LOINC) but the protocol-context dimensions that make a glucose value interpretable live elsewhere in the SDTM model — or in study assembly logic that is not part of CDISC standards.

Summary. COSMoS does case-construction work where the dimensions are fixed by the test (8 DSSs for the specimen × method × scale matrix). It deliberately does not encode dimensions that vary by study protocol (timing, challenge, visit).

A clinically interpretable glucose case = DSS recording template + per-row instance values + study-context assembly. The first piece is standardised; the third is not.

Part 2
Specializations of GLUCPL — what a study designer authors on top of the template

A study designer does not author GLUCPL. They author cases that specialize it — more-constrained templates in which some of GLUCPL's open slots are pinned to specific values, and some of the outside-DSS dimensions from Part 1 are bound to study-design objects (trial events, timepoint references, activities).

Each case is still a schema, not an instance: it describes every observation that matches the case, across every subject and visit.

Three illustrative specializations are shown below. The case_id values (ID001, ID002, ID003) are purely syntactic placeholders — the architectural point is that such IDs would need to be governed by a layer that does not yet exist.

What the three cases show together. ID001 is the clean case — everything resolves inside the DSS.

ID002 and ID003 cross the DSS boundary: both require a stable reference to the same OGTT challenge event, but pin LBFAST differently because they sample before vs after the load.

The outside-DSS object (the challenge itself) is authored per protocol and is not today a CDISC-governed registrable entity — each protocol re-authors it.

Cases ID002 and ID003 compose GLUCPL with that external reference; the identity of a case is a binding across layers, not a single pointer.

Registry need
A case-specialization registry

Every sponsor catalogue observed in practice holds entries equivalent to the three cases above — FPG, OGTT variants, glucose measurements differentiated by specimen and by clinical use — but as text strings in spec documents and eCRF libraries, not governed identifiers.

The anchor they pin onto is already governed: GLUCPL (DS_Code / vlm_group_id), C105585 (NCIt / COSMoS BC), and the surrounding SDTM CT come with curation, stable IDs, and release-versioned provenance.

The gap sits one layer up — at the case-specialization layer — where study-design intent lives today as sponsor-local strings with no shared vocabulary. The syntactic IDs used here (ID001, ID002, ID003) mark the slot a governed cross-sponsor layer would fill.

A case-specialization registry would assign each case a stable ID, carry its inside-DSS pins and outside-DSS bindings, and give study designers and downstream reviewers a single governed namespace to address intent across sponsor boundaries.
